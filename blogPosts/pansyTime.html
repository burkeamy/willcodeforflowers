Stop! Pansy Time

Oct. 26, 2020

Sometimes it is really hard to pull up those fabulous looking summer annuals and replace them. Trust me, as soon as the first frost hits, you will be glad you did!

Where I live, pansies and violas are the only annuals that will consistently bloom all winter long. They may be small in stature, but they come I a wide variety of colors and bloom sizes. Violas have a smaller flower but require less deadheading. My observation is that the matrix variety of pansy offers the largest bloom.

We plant pansy beds of all sizes this time of year. Some only hold one flat or about 15-20 individual plants. Then there are the big beds that hold 20 flats. That is 360 plants. This isn't even big by some standards. How do we do it? I'm glad you asked. Here is our process.

My pansy planting process

  1. Pull out the old summer annuals.
  2. Rake out any debris left in the bed
  3. Add a soil supplement. We use a cow manure product
  4. Till the soil and rake the bed to make it smooth
  5. Lay-out the plants to make sure you like the way it looks
  6. plant them making sure to break the roots open if needed
  7. Add a fertilizer
  8. Mulch lightly with a pine nugget mix
  9. Net them if deer are present
  10. Water
  11. Enjoy

Protecting your plants from deer is a crucial step. Pansies are like candy to deer. If left open and untreated, they will get pulled out and eaten. If you are diligent, a deer spray can work. They must be applied often. Many of our clients are not able to do this, so we put a type of wildlife netting over them. Note, this does take away from the overall appearance. You may want to plant Dianthus or snapdragons instead. They will have fewer blooms throughout the winter but are more deer resistant.
